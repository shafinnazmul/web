🧗 Warm-up Glossary — The Vocabulary You Need Before You Start

Kerberos has its own dictionary of jargon — much of it from cryptographic protocol design, much of it specific to MIT's 1980s naming conventions. None of these words are actually hard once you know what they refer to. This glossary unpacks the core eight terms that appear most often. If you're already comfortable with these, skip ahead to §1.

What is "authentication" (vs "authorization")? 📄 pp. 261-262, 294

These are two different concepts that students confuse all the time:

  • Authentication answers the question "who are you?" — it is the process of verifying a claimed identity. You authenticate when you log in with a password, scan a fingerprint, or present a Kerberos ticket. 📄 p. 261
  • Authorization answers a different question: "what are you allowed to do?" — it is the process of checking whether the authenticated identity has permission for a specific action. You're authorized when the file server checks whether your role permits reading /secret.txt. 📄 p. 271

Both happen on every secure access, but in this order: authenticate first, then authorize. In the OpenID/OAuth world, OpenID Connect is the authentication piece (issues an ID Token proving who you are) and OAuth 2.0 is the authorization piece (issues an Access Token proving what you're allowed to do).

What is a "key"?

In cryptography, a key is a secret number (typically 128-256 bits long) used by a cipher to encrypt and decrypt messages. In Kerberos, you'll meet two flavors:

  • Long-term key — a master secret tied to a specific entity (a user, a server, or the TGS itself). Created once during account setup, used for years. Stored carefully on the KDC and on the entity's own machine. Compromise of a long-term key is a serious breach.
  • Short-term session key — a fresh random key created on demand and discarded after a few hours. Used for a single conversation between two specific parties. Compromise of a session key only exposes that one session, and only for its remaining lifetime.

Kerberos uses long-term keys to bootstrap trust and short-term session keys for the actual encrypted communication. The pattern: "use the master key briefly to negotiate a session key, then use the session key for everything else." 📄 p. 303

What is a "ticket"? 📄 pp. 300, 320

A ticket in Kerberos is exactly what the name implies: a short proof-of-purchase that you flash at the door. Concretely, a ticket is an encrypted blob issued by the KDC that contains, among other things, a session key. The user receives the ticket but cannot read inside it (the encryption uses a key only the destination server knows). The user just hands the ticket over to the destination server, which decrypts it and reads the session key.

Two important properties:

  • Tickets are bearer tokens — anyone holding a valid ticket can present it. By itself, that's a problem (a thief could steal one and use it). Kerberos's solution is the authenticator (next entry).
  • Tickets are long-lived — typically 8-24 hours, matching a workday. This is what enables single sign-on: get one ticket in the morning, use it all day.

What is an "authenticator"? 📄 pp. 310, 321

An authenticator is a tiny, freshly-generated, single-use proof that you really are the rightful owner of a ticket — not a thief who stole it from somewhere. Concretely, the authenticator is a small encrypted blob containing your identity, your network address, and the current timestamp, encrypted with the session key embedded in the ticket.

Why does this prove you're the legitimate user? Because:

  1. The session key is buried inside the encrypted ticket, which only the destination server can decrypt.
  2. But the rightful user also received the session key separately (in a different message, encrypted with their own long-term key).
  3. So only the rightful user knows the session key without going through the destination server.
  4. Producing a valid authenticator (encrypted under the session key) proves you have it — proving you are who you claim.

Authenticators have very short lifetimes (minutes, not hours) so even if intercepted they can't be replayed later. Ticket = passport (long-lived, reusable). Authenticator = fresh selfie (short-lived, single-use). You need both.

What is the "KDC"? What are "AS" and "TGS"? 📄 pp. 302, 311

The Key Distribution Center (KDC) is the trusted server at the center of every Kerberos deployment. It holds the long-term keys of every user, every service, and itself. It's the equivalent of the front desk at a hotel.

Inside the KDC, two functional components work together:

  • Authentication Server (AS) — the part that verifies your password (once per day) and hands you a "TGS ticket." Think of it as the front desk that checks your ID at the start of your stay.
  • Ticket-Granting Service (TGS) — the part that issues per-service tickets after you've already been authenticated by the AS. Think of it as the concierge who hands out specific service vouchers (pool, spa, gym) for the rest of your stay.

In real Kerberos deployments, AS and TGS run on the same physical machine — they're separated conceptually so the password is only used once per day (with AS), not once per service request.

What is "single sign-on" (SSO)? 📄 pp. 270, 306

Single sign-on means: "the user enters their password once, and that one authentication grants access to many services without further password entry." Kerberos pioneered this concept in the 1980s. OpenID Connect implements the same idea over the web — log in once at login.microsoft.com, and all of Office 365 (Outlook, Calendar, Teams, OneDrive) just works without re-prompting.

SSO is what makes the difference between "many-to-one authentication" (one service, one password) and "many-to-many authentication" (many services, one shared sign-on). Both Kerberos and OpenID solve the many-to-many problem, just with different cryptographic foundations.

What is a "JWT" (JSON Web Token)? 📄 p. 272

A JWT is the data format used by modern web tokens (ID Tokens and Access Tokens). It's a self-contained string with three parts separated by dots: 

   JWT structure:   header.payload.signature

   Header     = { "alg": "RS256", "typ": "JWT" }              ← algorithm metadata
   Payload    = { "sub": "alice@example.com",                 ← claims about the user
                  "iss": "https://login.example.com",
                  "exp": 1740000000 }
   Signature  = RSA_sign(private_key, header + "." + payload) ← proves authenticity

The whole thing is base64-encoded so it survives in HTTP headers and URLs. The signature at the end proves the token came from the legitimate issuer — anyone with the issuer's public key can verify it without contacting the server. This is what makes JWTs self-contained and stateless: the server doesn't need to look up a session in a database; the token carries everything it needs.

What is "implicit" vs "explicit" authentication? 📄 p. 310

Two ways of proving who you are:

  • Implicit authentication — proof by ability. If you can decrypt a message encrypted with your key, you must know the key, so you must be the legitimate owner. No explicit "I am Alice, here's my proof" message — the proof is built into the act of being able to read what was sent. Example: when AS encrypts the TGS reply with K_c, only Alice (who knows K_c, derived from her password) can decrypt it. Decrypting successfully is the proof.
  • Explicit authentication — proof by reply. You receive a challenge (e.g., a timestamp), modify it in a specific way (e.g., add 1), encrypt it with the session key, and send it back. The recipient verifies. Example: at the end of the Kerberos exchange, the service server sends back E_K_c,v(TS+1) — explicitly telling the client "yes, I'm the real server."

Most of the Kerberos exchange uses implicit authentication; only the final mutual-auth message (Message 6) is explicit.

§1 — What & Why, in Plain English

📘 LECTURE PDF COVERAGE: pp. 261-272, 294-302, 311 (the conceptual foundation before diving into mechanism)

1.1 The fundamental problem — proving you are who you say you are 📄 p. 261

Imagine you walk up to a bank teller you've never met. You say "I'm Alice, I have $5,000 in account #12345, I'd like to withdraw $1,000." The teller asks: "How do I know you're really Alice?" That's the user authentication problem.

In the physical world, the teller would ask for a driver's license — a document with your photo, signature, and a state-issued seal that's hard to forge. In the digital world, you don't have a photo to show; you have only bits over a network. The challenge is to prove you know some secret that only the real Alice would know, without leaking that secret to anyone listening on the line.

Slide 261 frames it crisply: "How do you prove to someone that you are who you claim to be? Any system with access control must solve this problem." Email logins, banking websites, SSH connections, Wi-Fi, building keycards — every single one is a user authentication system. 📄 p. 261

1.2 Three ways to prove identity 📄 p. 266

The classical taxonomy from slide 266 — every authentication mechanism in the world boils down to one or more of these three categories:

CategoryPlain-English meaningExamplesStrengths / Weaknesses
Something you know A secret only you would know. Password, PIN, security questions ("what's your mother's maiden name?") Cheap; easily forgotten or guessed; vulnerable to phishing.
Something you have A physical object only you possess. Phone (for SMS codes), hardware token (YubiKey), smart card. Hard to steal at scale; can be lost; requires distribution.
Something you are A biometric trait unique to your body. Fingerprint, face ID, iris scan, voice. Convenient; can't be "changed" if compromised; sensor-spoofable.

Combine two of these and you have two-factor authentication (2FA). The combination matters: any single factor on its own is breakable. Passwords get phished, phones get stolen, biometrics get bypassed. But getting all three at once is hard for an attacker — they need both your password AND physical access to your phone, for example.

1.3 Many-to-one vs many-to-many authentication 📄 pp. 270, 294

This is the architectural split that organizes the whole topic. There are two scenarios:

Many-to-one — N users authenticating to ONE service

Many users log in to one specific server. Examples:

  • Many students logging into myBama.ua.edu
  • Many developers logging into one SSH server
  • Many shoppers visiting amazon.com

This is the simple case. The server holds a list of users (or a public-key database, or a CA trust anchor). Solutions: password + cookies, X.509 certificates with HTTPS, public-key SSH. All of §2a (Many-to-One) addresses this. 📄 pp. 263-269, 294

Many-to-many — N users authenticating to M services

Now imagine you're at a university with 30 different services (email, file storage, library, parking, course-management, payroll, ...) and 10,000 users. The naive solution — every server knows every user's password — is a disaster:

  • Insecure — break into one weakly-secured server, you compromise every user's password (because that one server knows all of them). 📄 p. 270
  • Inefficient — to change your password, you have to contact 30 different servers individually. 📄 p. 270
  • Inconvenient — users have to log in 30 different times, once per service.

The fix: centralize authentication. One trusted authentication system handles all logins, and every service just trusts it instead of holding its own password database. The course teaches two architecturally different solutions to many-to-many auth:

  • OpenID Connect + OAuth 2.0 — web-based, uses public-key cryptography, JWT tokens, and a web of trust anchored in CAs. Good for cross-organization SSO (your Google account works on Spotify, Pinterest, Stack Overflow, ...). 📄 p. 298
  • Kerberos — enterprise-based, uses symmetric cryptography only, ticket-based. Good for inside one organization (Microsoft Active Directory uses Kerberos for everything). 📄 pp. 298, 311

1.4 Why does Kerberos use only symmetric crypto? 📄 pp. 298, 311

This is the most-asked question in the deep-dive material. Modern crypto provides both symmetric (fast, single-key) and asymmetric (slow, key-pair) options. Why does Kerberos restrict itself to symmetric only?

Two reasons, the first historical and the second architectural:

  1. Historical: Kerberos was designed at MIT in the 1980s, before public-key cryptography was practical to deploy. Slide 300 says it directly: "Hash functions would've worked well, but this is 1980s design." Public-key infrastructure (PKI) didn't exist; RSA was patented and slow; certificates and CAs weren't standardized. Symmetric crypto was the only option that could ship in real software. 📄 p. 300
  2. Architectural: Kerberos sits inside a single trust domain (a corporate network, a university campus). Inside such a domain, there's already a centralized authority (the IT department) that can pre-share long-term keys with every user and server out-of-band. With that pre-existing trust relationship, you don't need public-key crypto — symmetric works fine and runs much faster. Public-key crypto was invented to solve the harder problem of establishing trust between strangers (which is what TLS does on the open Internet). 📄 pp. 87, 311

The tradeoff: Kerberos is fast, simple, and works inside a trust domain. It struggles to scale across organizations (cross-realm trust requires careful manual setup) — which is exactly where OpenID Connect with PKI shines.

1.5 The hotel concierge analogy — Kerberos in plain language 📄 pp. 300, 302, 306-308

Kerberos is intimidating because it has many moving parts (3 servers, 5 keys, 6 messages). But the architecture maps cleanly onto a familiar physical scenario: a guest at a hotel with a concierge service. Hold this picture firmly in your head and the rest of Topic 5 becomes much easier.

The whole protocol's elegance: you type your password once at check-in, and every service you use afterward is authenticated without typing it again. This is the "single logon" property — the same idea OpenID Connect implements for the web. 📄 p. 306

2aMany-to-One Authentication — with worked examples 📄 pp. 263-269 · Lecture 11 part 1

The simplest case — passwords 📄 pp. 268-269

Intuition

You have an account on a server. The server knows your username and a stored representation of your password. When you log in, you type the password; the server compares what you typed to what it stored. If they match, you're authenticated.

Three sub-questions the lecture asks 📄 p. 268

QuestionNaive answerWhat's wrongModern fix
How is the password communicated? Send it in the clear (HTTP) Anyone sniffing the network reads your password. Always send over HTTPS (TLS-encrypted).
How is the password stored? Plaintext in the database Database breach exposes every user's password. Store H(password + salt) — a salted hash. Even if breached, attackers can't recover originals without brute force.
How is the password checked? Compare strings directly "Wrong" if even a single character mismatches — that's fine, but timing-attack vulnerabilities possible. Constant-time string comparison + rate limiting on attempts.

The fundamental weakness of passwords

The instructor put it bluntly on slide 268: "Easy-to-remember passwords tend to be easy to guess." Humans pick predictable passwords (123456, password123, names, dates). Attackers build dictionaries of millions of common passwords and try them all (credential stuffing). The defense: two-factor authentication — combine the password with something else (your phone, a hardware key). Now the attacker needs both. 📄 pp. 267, 344

Web-Auth 1.0 — cookie-based session management 📄 p. 269

The problem cookies solve

HTTP is stateless: each request to a website is independent, and the server has no built-in memory of who you are between requests. Without some mechanism, you'd have to type your password before every single click — completely unusable.

How cookies work

Why cookies don't scale to many-to-many 📄 p. 269

Cookies are tied to a specific domain. The cookie set by email.example.com is only sent back to email.example.com, not to files.example.com. So if you have 30 services, you log in 30 times, and each issues its own cookie. Cross-domain SSO is impossible with simple cookies — that's exactly why we needed Web-Auth 2.0 (token-based, with OpenID Connect and OAuth).

Public-key authentication — SSH and GitHub 📄 pp. 265-268

Intuition

Instead of sharing a password with the server, you generate a cryptographic key pair on your laptop. You give the server only your public key; you keep your private key safe. When the server wants to verify you, it gives you a random challenge; you sign it with your private key; the server checks the signature using your public key. Because only you have the private key, only you could have produced a valid signature.

Worked example — SSH login 📄 p. 265

  SSH PUBLIC-KEY AUTHENTICATION                  📄 p. 265

  Setup (once):
    Alice generates a keypair on her laptop:
      ssh-keygen → produces id_rsa (private) + id_rsa.pub (public)
    Alice copies id_rsa.pub to the server's ~/.ssh/authorized_keys file.

  Login (every time):

  Alice's laptop                       Remote server
  ──────────────                       ────────────────
  ── 1. ssh alice@server ──►
                                       Look up alice's public key in
                                       ~alice/.ssh/authorized_keys.
  ◄── 2. server's host key + ────────
        challenge nonce R
                                       (R is a random 256-bit value)

  Sign R with private key:
     s = Sign_sk(R)
  ── 3. response: s ──►
                                       Verify signature using Alice's
                                       public key:
                                          Verify_pk(s, R) = TRUE ?
                                       If yes, login succeeds.
  ◄── 4. shell prompt ──────────────

The GitHub variation 📄 p. 266

GitHub uses essentially the same protocol for git push/pull operations, but the registration is even more elegant: you register the SHA-256 hash of your public key in your GitHub account settings. When you push code, your SSH client signs a challenge; GitHub verifies using the pre-registered public-key fingerprint. You never type a password — the cryptographic signature itself proves you're you. 📄 pp. 266-268

Why this is better than passwords

  • Server breach reveals nothing useful. If GitHub's database is stolen, attackers get a list of public keys — but public keys are public by design. They can't impersonate you without your private key, which never left your laptop.
  • Phishing-resistant. A fake server can't get anything useful from you because the only thing you ever send is a signature on a random challenge, useless without context.
  • No "password reset" by accident. Lose your laptop = lose the key = generate a new one and re-register. No social-engineering attacks via "forgot password" flows.

X.509 certificate authentication — HTTPS 📄 p. 263

This is the dual of public-key SSH: instead of you proving identity to the server, the server proves identity to you. The server has an X.509 certificate signed by a Certificate Authority (CA). Your browser comes pre-loaded with a list of trusted CAs. When you visit bankofamerica.com, the server presents its certificate, your browser checks the CA signature, and if valid, you trust the server's public key.

After this, the actual user authentication usually still happens via password+cookie (HTTPS carries those securely) or OpenID Connect. The X.509 step authenticates the server; separate steps authenticate the user.

2bOpenID Connect & OAuth 2.0 — with worked examples 📄 pp. 271-291 · Lecture 11 part 2

Why we needed Web-Auth 2.0 📄 pp. 270-272

Recall the problem: cookies don't cross domains. So if you have an account on Outlook (Microsoft) and an account on Pinterest (Cinemos Inc), the two are entirely separate. Wouldn't it be nice if you could log in once at login.microsoft.com and Outlook + OneDrive + Teams all just worked?

That's the goal of token-based authentication (Web-Auth 2.0). The key idea: instead of each service maintaining its own session in its own database, an identity provider issues a self-contained, cryptographically signed token that any service can verify on its own. The token IS the proof of identity.

The JWT format unpacked 📄 p. 272

A JSON Web Token looks like one long opaque string, but it's actually three base64-encoded JSON pieces joined by dots: 

  JWT (JSON Web Token) STRUCTURE                  📄 p. 272

  Wire format:    eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIs...   .  eyJzdWIiOiJhbGljZSIs...   .  rk_lYV6ZmTl...
                  ───────────────────────       ───────────────────────       ───────────
                       Header                          Payload                  Signature

  After base64 decoding:

   Header  = {                              ← describes the token format
     "alg": "RS256",                          (which signature algorithm)
     "typ": "JWT"
   }

   Payload = {                              ← the actual claims about the user
     "sub": "alice@example.com",              (subject: who is this token about)
     "iss": "https://login.example.com",      (issuer: who created the token)
     "aud": "outlook-client-id",              (audience: who is this token for)
     "exp": 1740000000,                       (expiration time)
     "iat": 1739913600,                       (issued at time)
     "name": "Alice Example",
     "email": "alice@example.com"
   }

   Signature = RSA_sign( issuer_private_key,
                         base64(header) + "." + base64(payload) )

  Properties:
    ★ Self-contained: anyone with issuer's public key can verify the signature.
    ★ No database lookup needed — claims are right there in the payload.
    ★ Tamper-evident: change one bit, signature breaks.
    ★ Time-limited: exp claim ensures expired tokens are rejected.

OpenID vs OAuth — the critical distinction 📄 pp. 272, 298

This is the most-confused pair in the entire course. The instructor was crystal clear:

AspectOpenID ConnectOAuth 2.0
Question it answers "Who is this user?" "What can this app do on the user's behalf?"
Token issued ID Token (JWT with identity claims) Access Token (JWT with scope permissions)
Used for Single sign-on, user authentication Authorization, API access delegation
Real-world example "Sign in with Google" on Spotify "Allow Mint to read my Chase account"
Built on OAuth 2.0 (extends it with identity claims) Standalone protocol
The mnemonic that finally makes it stick OpenID has "ID" in the name → identity → who you are.
OAuth contains "auth" → authorization → what you may do.
Both are JWTs, both ride the same Authorization Code Flow, but they answer fundamentally different questions. 📄 p. 298

① The setup phase — what happens before any login 📄 pp. 302, 303

Before Alice can ever use Kerberos, three setup steps must happen — typically by an IT admin during initial deployment. Understanding setup is key to understanding why Kerberos can use symmetric crypto only.

Setup ①: Alice gets an account; KDC stores K_c 📄 p. 303
Admin creates an account for Alice in the Kerberos database. Alice picks a password. The KDC computes K_c = hash(Alice's password) and stores K_c in its database (NOT the password — never the password). The same hash function is later run on Alice's laptop at login time to reproduce K_c locally without the KDC ever sending it.

Why hash, not store the password directly? If the KDC database is ever breached, attackers get only K_c values — not reusable plaintext passwords (defense in depth). Modern Kerberos uses PBKDF2 or similar key-derivation functions, not a plain hash, to slow down brute-force attacks against the stored values.
Setup ②: Each service V gets its own random K_v 📄 p. 303
Admin generates a random secret key K_v specifically for each service V (file server, print server, email, etc.). K_v is stored in two places:
  1. The KDC database, alongside V's service record;
  2. Server V's own machine, installed by the sysadmin out-of-band (e.g., physically copied during machine setup).
K_v is not derived from a password — it's purely random, typically 256 bits of entropy. Each service has its own completely independent K_v. The mail server's K_v leaking has zero effect on the file server's K_v.
Setup ③: AS and TGS share an internal K_TGS 📄 p. 303
The KDC contains two functional components, AS and TGS, that need to share a secret. This is K_TGS — typically just an internal key on the same physical machine. K_TGS is what allows the AS to "seal" TGS tickets so that only the TGS can open them.

After setup, the entity-key map looks like this (read this very carefully — it's the foundation of everything that follows): 

  WHO KNOWS WHAT — AFTER SETUP, BEFORE LOGIN     📄 p. 303

   ┌─────────────────────┐         ┌─────────────────────┐         ┌─────────────────────┐
   │      Alice (C)      │         │      KDC (AS+TGS)   │         │   Service V         │
   │  ─────────────────  │         │  ─────────────────  │         │  ─────────────────  │
   │  K_c = hash(pwd)    │         │  K_c (Alice's)      │         │  K_v (her own)      │
   │  (computed at login)│         │  K_c (Bob's)        │         │                     │
   │                     │         │  K_c (Carol's) ...  │         │                     │
   │                     │         │  K_TGS              │         │                     │
   │                     │         │  K_v (file server)  │         │                     │
   │                     │         │  K_v (print server) │         │                     │
   │                     │         │  K_v (email)   ...  │         │                     │
   └─────────────────────┘         └─────────────────────┘         └─────────────────────┘

   Alice does NOT know K_v.  Service V does NOT know K_c.  Neither knows the other exists yet.
   Only the KDC has the central view that allows it to mediate between them.

② The trust model — who trusts whom 📄 p. 304

Slide 304 makes this point with three crisp statements:

  • Clients and Service Servers trust the KDC (AS + TGS). This is the central trust assumption — the KDC is the authority everyone defers to. Compromise the KDC, compromise everything.
  • The KDC does NOT trust clients or servers. It treats them as potentially adversarial — every claim must be verified cryptographically.
  • Clients and Service Servers do NOT trust each other. Alice and the printer have no reason to believe each other; everything they exchange must be vouched for by the KDC.

This three-way mutual mistrust (with KDC as referee) is what makes Kerberos's complicated dance necessary. If Alice and the printer already trusted each other, they could just talk directly.

③ The five keys — re-introduced with full detail 📄 p. 303

We met these in the warm-up glossary. Now look at them with fresh eyes — pay attention to the subscript count rule: one entity in the subscript = long-term; two entities = session.

Key Type Created by Lifetime Held by Purpose
K_c Long-term Derived from Alice's password Years Alice + KDC Encrypts Message 2 so only Alice can read it. Used only at initial login (once per day).
K_TGS Long-term Admin (random) Years AS + TGS (both inside KDC) Encrypts every TGS ticket so only TGS can decrypt it. Internal KDC secret.
K_v Long-term Admin (random, one per service) Years Service V + TGS Encrypts every service ticket so only V can decrypt it.
K_c,TGS Session AS (fresh random) Hours (workday) Alice + TGS Protects all client ↔ TGS communication after initial login.
K_c,v Session TGS (fresh random) Hours Alice + Service V Protects the actual application session between Alice and V.
The subscript trick Whenever you see a Kerberos key on the exam, look at how many things are in the subscript:
  • One entity (K_c, K_v, K_TGS) → it's a long-term key.
  • Two entities separated by a comma (K_c,TGS, K_c,v) → it's a session key.
This was the instructor's exact rule-of-thumb in the review. 📄 p. 303

④ The six messages — walked one at a time 📄 pp. 305-308

Now the centerpiece. We'll trace Alice's full Kerberos session — login at 8 AM, then access the file server at 10 AM. Six messages happen across the day. Each is unpacked field-by-field with annotations on which key encrypts it and why.

📨 Message 1: Alice → AS — "Please give me a TGS ticket" 📄 p. 306

  MESSAGE 1: Alice's first request, 8 AM         📄 pp. 306, 318

  Alice's laptop                                  AS (inside KDC)
  ────────────────                                ────────────────
  Alice types her password.
  Laptop computes K_c locally (hash of password).

  Builds Message 1:
       ID_c   = "alice@REALM.COM"
       ID_TGS = "krbtgt/REALM.COM"  (the TGS's name)
       TS_1   = current timestamp

  ────── (M1) ID_c, ID_TGS, TS_1 ──────────►
  (sent in the CLEAR — no encryption!)
                                                  AS receives M1.
                                                  Looks up Alice's K_c
                                                  in the KDC database.
                                                  AS now has K_c in memory.
Why is M1 sent unencrypted? Isn't that insecure? No. M1 contains no secrets — just identifiers. Alice's password is NOT in M1. The fact that someone is requesting a TGS ticket "as alice" is fine; the AS will only respond with something Alice can decrypt (using K_c, which the attacker doesn't have). An eavesdropper learns "alice is logging in at 8 AM" — that's metadata, not a credential. 📄 pp. 306, 318

📨 Message 2: AS → Alice — TGS ticket bundled inside two layers 📄 p. 306

This is the most complex message in the entire protocol. Read it slowly; we'll unpack each field below. 

  MESSAGE 2: AS's reply to Alice                 📄 pp. 306, 318

  AS does several things in sequence:

  (a) Generate a fresh session key K_c,TGS (random).
  (b) Build the inner Ticket_TGS:
        Ticket_TGS = Encrypt_K_TGS{
                        K_c,TGS,         ← session key, copy for TGS
                        ID_c,            ← Alice's identity
                        AD_c,            ← Alice's network address
                        ID_TGS,
                        TS_2,            ← time AS issued this
                        Lifetime_2       ← how long the ticket is valid
                     }
        ↑ Encrypted with K_TGS, so only TGS can decrypt it.
        ↑ Alice will receive this but CAN'T READ inside.

  (c) Wrap the outer message:
        M2 = Encrypt_K_c{
                K_c,TGS,                 ← session key, copy for Alice
                ID_TGS,
                TS_2,
                Lifetime_2,
                Ticket_TGS               ← the opaque blob from step (b)
             }
        ↑ Encrypted with K_c, so only Alice can decrypt the outer wrapper.

  ◄────── M2 ─────────────────────────────

  Alice's laptop:
  - Decrypts outer M2 with K_c (derived from her password).
  - Successful decryption proves she knows the password (implicit auth).
  - Extracts K_c,TGS for her own use.
  - Holds Ticket_TGS as an opaque blob she'll relay to TGS later.
  - Discards K_c from memory (no need to keep it).
The "envelope-in-envelope" pattern M2 has two encryption layers because it carries two pieces of information for two different parties:
Outer layer (K_c) → for Alice. Tells her: "here's K_c,TGS, and here's an opaque ticket to give TGS."
Inner layer (K_TGS) → for TGS, but Alice carries it. Tells TGS: "I (the AS) authorize the holder of K_c,TGS — namely Alice — to use the TGS service."
The same K_c,TGS appears in both layers — so both Alice and TGS end up knowing it, but only after Alice relays the ticket. 📄 p. 306

📨 Message 3: Alice → TGS — "I want a service ticket for the printer" 📄 p. 307

Hours later, Alice wants to print. She needs a service ticket for the print server. She presents her TGS ticket plus a fresh authenticator: 

  MESSAGE 3: Alice asks TGS for a service ticket  📄 pp. 307, 319

  Alice still has from M2: K_c,TGS and Ticket_TGS.

  She builds a fresh Authenticator:
        Authenticator_c = Encrypt_K_c,TGS{
                              ID_c,        ← her identity
                              AD_c,        ← her network address
                              TS_3         ← current time (FRESH!)
                          }
        ↑ Encrypted with K_c,TGS, so only someone who knows that key
          (i.e., either Alice or TGS) could have produced it.

  Builds M3:
        M3 = (
            ID_v,            ← which service she wants (e.g., "print")
            Ticket_TGS,      ← her TGS ticket from M2 (still encrypted with K_TGS)
            Authenticator_c  ← fresh proof she's the rightful holder
        )

  ────── M3 ─────────────────────────────►
                                              TGS receives M3.
                                              (a) Decrypts Ticket_TGS using K_TGS.
                                                  Reveals K_c,TGS, ID_c, AD_c, lifetime.
                                              (b) Uses K_c,TGS to decrypt Authenticator_c.
                                                  Reveals ID_c, AD_c, TS_3.
                                              (c) VERIFIES:
                                                  - ID_c in ticket == ID_c in authenticator?
                                                  - AD_c in ticket == AD_c in authenticator?
                                                  - TS_3 is fresh (within minutes of TGS clock)?
                                                  - Ticket lifetime not expired?
                                              All checks pass → Alice is authenticated to TGS.
Why the authenticator and ticket BOTH? Why not just one? Without the authenticator, an attacker who sniffs Ticket_TGS off the wire could replay it as if they were Alice. The ticket itself is encrypted with K_TGS — the attacker can't read it, but they can copy it. The authenticator defeats this: it's encrypted with K_c,TGS, which the attacker doesn't have (only Alice and TGS, after TGS decrypts the ticket). So producing a valid authenticator proves the presenter knows K_c,TGS, which means they're the rightful Alice. 📄 p. 310

📨 Message 4: TGS → Alice — service ticket for the printer 📄 p. 307

Same pattern as Message 2: TGS issues a new session key K_c,v and a new ticket, both wrapped in a layer Alice can read using K_c,TGS. 

  MESSAGE 4: TGS gives Alice a service ticket    📄 pp. 307, 319-320

  TGS:
  (a) Generates a fresh session key K_c,v (random).
  (b) Builds inner Ticket_v:
        Ticket_v = Encrypt_K_v{
                      K_c,v,           ← session key, copy for V
                      ID_c,
                      AD_c,
                      ID_v,
                      TS_4,
                      Lifetime_4
                   }
        ↑ Encrypted with K_v, so only V can decrypt it.

  (c) Builds outer M4:
        M4 = Encrypt_K_c,TGS{
                K_c,v,                ← session key, copy for Alice
                ID_v,
                TS_4,
                Lifetime_4,
                Ticket_v              ← the opaque blob
             }
        ↑ Encrypted with K_c,TGS so only Alice can decrypt the wrapper.

  ◄────── M4 ─────────────────────────────

  Alice:
  - Decrypts outer M4 with K_c,TGS.
  - Extracts K_c,v for her own use.
  - Holds Ticket_v as an opaque blob she'll relay to V.

Notice the parallel structure with M2 — same envelope-in-envelope pattern, just one layer removed from the password (uses K_c,TGS instead of K_c). This is the key architectural insight: K_c is used only at login; everything afterward uses session keys.

📨 Message 5: Alice → V — service request 📄 p. 308

Same pattern as M3, with one layer further removed: 

  MESSAGE 5: Alice presents service ticket to V  📄 pp. 308, 322

  Alice builds a fresh Authenticator (NEW one, different from M3's):
        Authenticator_c = Encrypt_K_c,v{
                              ID_c,
                              AD_c,
                              TS_5      ← fresh timestamp
                          }

  Builds M5:
        M5 = (Ticket_v, Authenticator_c)

  ────── M5 ─────────────────────────────►
                                              V receives M5.
                                              (a) Decrypts Ticket_v with K_v.
                                                  Reveals K_c,v, ID_c, AD_c, lifetime.
                                              (b) Uses K_c,v to decrypt Authenticator_c.
                                              (c) VERIFIES same way as TGS did.
                                              Alice is now authenticated to V.

                                              Service can begin.

📨 Message 6: V → Alice — mutual authentication 📄 p. 308

Up to this point, V has authenticated Alice — V knows it's really Alice. But Alice does NOT yet know V is the real V (it could be an imposter who somehow rerouted her traffic). M6 closes the loop: 

  MESSAGE 6: V proves to Alice he is the real V  📄 pp. 308, 310

  V takes the timestamp Alice sent in the authenticator, adds 1 to it,
  encrypts the result with K_c,v:

        M6 = Encrypt_K_c,v{ TS_5 + 1 }

  ◄────── M6 ─────────────────────────────

  Alice:
  - Decrypts M6 with K_c,v.
  - Sees a value that equals TS_5 + 1.
  - Reasons: "Whoever sent this knew K_c,v. K_c,v was inside Ticket_v.
              Ticket_v was encrypted with K_v. Only the real V knows K_v.
              Therefore, this is the real V."
The chain of reasoning that makes mutual auth work Alice's logic is exactly what slide 308 spells out:
  1. "Server can produce M6 only if he knows K_c,v."
  2. "Server can learn K_c,v only if he can decrypt Ticket_v."
  3. "Server can decrypt Ticket_v only if he knows K_v."
  4. "If server knows K_v, then he is the real V."
Why TS_5 + 1, not just TS_5? If V echoed TS_5 unchanged, Alice couldn't tell whether V actually decrypted it or just replayed her own message back. Adding 1 (any deterministic transformation) proves V did the work — he had to know K_c,v to recover TS_5, add 1, re-encrypt. 📄 pp. 308, 310

⑤ The "implicit vs explicit" map of the entire flow 📄 p. 310

Now you can see the pattern across all six messages. Most of Kerberos uses implicit authentication; only M6 is explicit:

StepAuth typeWho proves what to whomBy what mechanism
M2 received Implicit Client → AS (in effect) Client successfully decrypts M2 with K_c → proves she knew the password
M3 sent Implicit Client → TGS Client produces fresh authenticator under K_c,TGS → proves she has the session key
M5 sent Implicit Client → V Client produces fresh authenticator under K_c,v → proves she has the service session key
M6 received Explicit (mutual) V → Client V echoes back TS_5+1 encrypted under K_c,v → proves V really has K_v
2dWhy Each Element Exists — Ablation Analysis 📄 pp. 314-322 · Lecture 12 part 2 + Lecture 13

Now that we've seen the full Kerberos protocol, the question becomes: why does it have so many parts? The instructor's pedagogical move on slides 314-316 is to present two simplified versions of Kerberos that are missing key elements, and let you see what attacks each version is vulnerable to. By comparing the simplifications to the full protocol, you learn what each element does.

Simplified Kerberos #1 — no TGS, password sent every time 📄 p. 315

Imagine Kerberos without a TGS, where Alice authenticates directly to AS for every service request. Each time she wants to access V, she sends her password again:

ProsCons
Ticket is still encrypted, can't be altered by Alice or attackers. Password must be sent to AS for every service request. If Alice wants to use 30 services in a day, she sends her password 30 times — each one a phishing/sniffing opportunity.
Different AD_c (network address) means an attacker on a different machine can't reuse the ticket. No ticket reuse. Alice has to re-authenticate from scratch every time, defeating single sign-on.

What's missing: the TGS. Adding TGS lets Alice get a long-lived TGS ticket once per day; subsequent service requests reuse that ticket without re-sending the password. This is what enables single sign-on. 📄 p. 315

Simplified Kerberos #2 — TGS added, but no authenticator and no mutual auth 📄 pp. 316, 354

ProsCons
Password sent only once. TGS ticket reused. No mutual authentication between C and SS. Alice doesn't know whether she's talking to the real V or an imposter.
Single-sign-on works. Replay attack: attacker who captures Ticket_v can replay it later to access the service.
No session key for application-layer communication — anything Alice and V exchange afterward is in the clear.

What's missing: the authenticator (defeats replay) and Message 6 (mutual auth). Adding the authenticator means each ticket use requires fresh proof of session-key knowledge. Adding M6 means Alice can verify she's at the real V. 📄 p. 316

Remaining threats — what Kerberos still can't defend against 📄 p. 352

Even with full Kerberos, three classes of threats remain:

  • Ticket hijacking on shared workstations. If Alice and Bob share a computer (e.g., a lab workstation), Bob can read Alice's ticket file from disk and replay it. Network-address verification (AD_c) doesn't help because both share the same workstation. 📄 p. 352
  • Server misconfiguration / spoofing. If an attacker can route messages to themselves instead of the real server, mutual authentication usually catches this — unless the network is misconfigured to not enforce M6 verification. Misconfiguration is the real vulnerability. 📄 p. 352
  • Offline password guessing against K_c. Recall M2 is encrypted with K_c, derived from Alice's password. An eavesdropper who captures M2 can try every common password offline: for each guess, derive K_c, attempt to decrypt M2, see if it produces structured data. Modern Kerberos V5 mitigates this with "pre-authentication" extensions. 📄 pp. 306, 352

The takeaway — Kerberos as engineered defense in depth

Kerberos is complex because every element earns its place by defeating a specific attack. Strip any one out and a corresponding attack becomes possible:

  • Why two-level key hierarchy? → so password is used only once per day.
  • Why ticket encryption? → so client can't tamper with claimed identity.
  • Why authenticator? → so ticket can't be replayed.
  • Why timestamp in authenticator? → so authenticator itself can't be replayed.
  • Why M6? → so client knows it's the real server.
  • Why AD_c? → so stolen ticket can't be used from a different machine.
  • Why lifetime? → so old tickets eventually become invalid.

3.2 — Common Misconceptions & Traps

❌ MYTH: "OpenID Connect and OAuth do the same thing — they're just different brand names."

✅ REALITY: They solve different problems. OpenID Connect answers "who is this user?" and issues an ID Token (JWT identifying the user). OAuth 2.0 answers "what can this app do on behalf of the user?" and issues an Access Token (granting scoped API access). They're often used together — OpenID Connect is actually built on top of OAuth — but the tokens and their purposes are distinct. 📄 pp. 272, 298

🎯 EXAM TRAP: "OAuth 2.0 is primarily for user authentication" — FALSE. OAuth is for access delegation (authorization).

❌ MYTH: "Kerberos uses public-key cryptography like TLS does."

✅ REALITY: Kerberos uses symmetric cryptography only. The instructor called this out explicitly: "Relies exclusively on symmetric encryption, making no use of public-key encryption." This is a defining architectural choice — everything is based on shared secrets and a trusted KDC. The downside: scaling requires cross-realm trust relationships. 📄 pp. 298, 311

🎯 EXAM TRAP: "What kind of key exchange does Kerberos use?" Correct answer: Kerberos distributes keys via a trusted third party (KDC), NOT via Diffie-Hellman or RSA.

❌ MYTH: "The ticket alone is enough to authenticate the client."

✅ REALITY: A ticket only proves "someone who obtained a ticket is presenting it" — it does not prove you are the one who obtained it. Anyone who intercepts a ticket on the wire could replay it. The authenticator (freshly generated, encrypted under the session key, with current timestamp) is what proves the presenter actually knows the session key embedded in the ticket — i.e., is the original holder. 📄 pp. 310, 316, 321

🎯 EXAM TRAP: Given the simplified Kerberos without authenticator, "What's the security flaw?" → Ticket replay attack.

❌ MYTH: "In Kerberos, the client and the service server share a long-term key directly."

✅ REALITY: Client and service V share only a short-term session key (K_c,v) created fresh by TGS for each session. Client has no long-term key with V — that's the whole point of the KDC mediation. The long-term keys K_c and K_v are both held with KDC, not with each other. 📄 pp. 303, 307

🎯 EXAM TRAP: Fill-in-the-blank or MCQ: "Which entities hold the long-term K_v?" → Only V and TGS/KDC. Not the client.

❌ MYTH: "Mutual authentication happens at the start of the Kerberos flow."

✅ REALITY: Mutual authentication is only achieved at Message 6 — when the service V replies with E_K_c,v(TS+1), proving it could decrypt Ticket_v (thus knows K_v, thus is the real V). Messages 1-5 are one-directional proofs from client to server. 📄 pp. 308, 310

🎯 EXAM TRAP: "At which step is mutual authentication achieved?" → Message 6, not Message 1 or 2.

❌ MYTH: "The TGS ticket expires quickly to enforce security."

✅ REALITY: The TGS ticket is deliberately long-lived (hours, typically a full workday) — that's what enables single sign-on. It's the authenticator that has a very short lifetime (seconds to a minute) to prevent replay. The ticket defines "you're authorized to request services"; the authenticator proves "you're doing so right now." 📄 pp. 306, 321, 322

🎯 EXAM TRAP: Do not conflate ticket lifetime with authenticator lifetime — they serve different purposes and have very different time scales.

▶ Show Answer

📝 Answer:

  • K_c — long-term. Held by Alice + KDC. (Subscript: one entity, "c" for client.)
  • K_TGS — long-term. Held by AS + TGS (both inside KDC). (One entity in subscript.)
  • K_v — long-term. Held by service V + TGS. (One entity in subscript.)

  • K_c,v — session. Held by client + V. (Two entities in subscript.)

WARM-UP Step-tracing 📄 pp. 306, 318

WC-3. In Kerberos Message 2, the AS sends Alice a reply with two layers of encryption: an outer layer encrypted with K_c, and an inner "ticket" encrypted with K_TGS. The same value — K_c,TGS — appears inside both layers. Why? What would break if K_c,TGS appeared in only one layer?

▶ Show Answer

📝 Answer: The two layers serve two different recipients:

  • Outer layer (K_c) is for Alice: she decrypts it with K_c (derived from her password) and learns K_c,TGS for her own use.
  • Inner ticket (K_TGS) is for TGS: it stays opaque to Alice (she can't read it), but she'll later relay it to TGS in Message 3. TGS decrypts with K_TGS and learns the same K_c,TGS, plus Alice's identity and her ticket lifetime.

Why both? Because Alice and TGS need to share K_c,TGS, but they have no existing channel to communicate directly — Alice doesn't know K_TGS, and TGS doesn't know K_c. The AS solves this by being the one party that knows both K_c and K_TGS, so it can encrypt the same K_c,TGS twice — once for each party. Alice serves as the courier for the TGS-encrypted half.

If K_c,TGS appeared only in the outer layer (encrypted with K_c), TGS would never learn it and couldn't communicate securely with Alice. If it appeared only in the inner layer, Alice would never learn it. The dual-encryption is the only way to deliver the same secret to two parties who don't share a key. 📄 pp. 306, 318

WARM-UP Threat reasoning 📄 pp. 310, 316, 321

WC-4. An attacker named Eve sniffs Alice's network and captures her Ticket_v (the encrypted service ticket from Message 4). Eve waits 5 minutes, then tries to use the captured ticket to access service V herself. Why does Kerberos prevent this attack? Be specific about which protocol element defeats it and why.

▶ Show Answer

📝 Answer: The authenticator defeats this replay attack. Even though Eve has the ticket bytes, when she presents them to V she also has to provide a fresh authenticator — a small encrypted blob containing the current timestamp, encrypted with K_c,v. To produce a valid authenticator, she'd need to know K_c,v.

But K_c,v is buried inside Ticket_v, which is encrypted with K_v — a key Eve doesn't have. Eve can copy the ticket bytes, but she can't read them. So she doesn't know K_c,v and can't produce a valid authenticator.

If Eve simply replays Alice's old authenticator (which she also captured), the timestamp inside it (TS_5 from Alice's original session) is now 5 minutes old. V verifies the timestamp is fresh (typically within ~1 minute of its own clock) and rejects the replayed authenticator. The combination of "authenticator encrypted with session key Eve doesn't have" + "fresh timestamp inside" is what makes ticket replay impossible. 📄 pp. 310, 316, 321

WARM-UP Conceptual 📄 pp. 272, 285-291, 298

WC-5. Your friend says: "I clicked 'Sign in with Google' on Spotify. Spotify now has my Google password — that's how it works, right?" Explain why your friend is wrong, and walk through what actually happens.

▶ Show Answer

📝 Answer: Spotify never sees the Google password. That's the entire point of OAuth + OpenID Connect. Walking through the actual flow:

  1. Click "Sign in with Google" on Spotify. Spotify redirects your browser to accounts.google.com/authorize.
  2. You log in at Google's website. Critically, this is the real Google domain — Spotify is not in the picture. Google verifies your password (and maybe MFA).
  3. Google asks for your consent: "Spotify wants to know your name and email. Allow / Deny?" You click Allow.
  4. Google redirects you back to Spotify with a short-lived authorization code in the URL.
  5. Spotify's server (not your browser) exchanges the code for tokens via a server-to-server call to Google. It receives an ID Token (proving who you are) and an Access Token (granting limited scope, like reading your email/name).
  6. Spotify's session is now associated with your Google identity, but Spotify only sees the claims in the ID Token (your sub, email, name). It does not see your password, and it cannot impersonate you to Google beyond the limited scope you granted.

If Spotify is later breached, attackers get only access tokens (limited scope, time-limited) — not your Google password. And you can revoke Spotify's access at any time from your Google account security settings, without changing your password.

This is why OAuth is one of the most important architectural innovations of the past 15 years: it lets you delegate specific limited access to third parties without sharing your master credential. 📄 pp. 272, 285-291, 298

Q5. Statement: Authentication and authorization are synonyms in modern identity systems — they both answer the question "Can this user perform this action?"

▶ Show Answer

✅ Answer: FALSE.

They are two distinct processes. Authentication answers "who are you?" — it verifies claimed identity. Authorization answers "what are you allowed to do?" — it checks whether the authenticated identity has permission for a specific action. Authentication must succeed first; only then can authorization apply its policies. Slide 294 states this verbatim: "Authentication: who are you. Authorization: what you are allowed to do." Conflating the two is one of the instructor's flagged traps. 📄 pp. 262, 294

INT TF 📄 pp. 306, 302

Q6. Statement: In Kerberos, the user must re-enter their password every time they request access to a new network service (printer, file server, email, etc.).

▶ Show Answer

✅ Answer: FALSE.

The defining feature of Kerberos is exactly the opposite — single logon. The user enters their password once per day (or per TGS-ticket lifetime) to the Authentication Server. After that, the client holds the TGS ticket and session key K_c,TGS; any subsequent service request (printer, email, file server) uses TGS to obtain a service ticket automatically, with no further password entry. Slide 306: "Client only needs to obtain TGS ticket once — say, every morning. Single logon — user enters password once per day." This separation of AS from TGS is precisely what enables single sign-on. 📄 pp. 302, 306

ADV · subtle TF 📄 pp. 308, 310

Q7. Statement: In Kerberos, mutual authentication between the client and the service server is established as soon as the client successfully presents its service ticket (Message 5), because the service can verify the ticket was signed by TGS.

▶ Show Answer

✅ Answer: FALSE.

Message 5 only authenticates the client to the service server. At that point, the service has verified the client knows K_c,v (because the client produced a valid authenticator), but the client has not yet verified it's talking to the real V. The client could be talking to an imposter who intercepted Message 5.
Mutual authentication is achieved only at Message 6, when V returns E_K_c,v(TS_5 + 1). By producing this reply, V proves it could decrypt Ticket_v — which requires K_v — which only the real V (and TGS) possesses. Slide 308 makes this explicit: "Authenticates server to client. Server can produce this message only if he knows key K_c,v. Server can learn K_c,v only if he can decrypt service ticket. Server can decrypt service ticket only if he knows correct key K_v. If server knows correct key K_v, then he is the right server." 📄 pp. 308, 310

Subtle trap: tickets are encrypted with K_v, not signed by TGS. There are no digital signatures anywhere in Kerberos — symmetric crypto only.

Q9. Consider a simplified version of Kerberos that omits the authenticator — clients present only the encrypted ticket to service servers. Explain what specific attack becomes possible against this design, and describe how the authenticator in full Kerberos defeats it.

▶ Show Answer

📝 Model Answer:

Without the authenticator, a ticket replay attack becomes possible. An attacker who sniffs a legitimate client-to-service exchange captures the encrypted ticket (Ticket_v). Even though the attacker cannot decrypt it (they don't know K_v), they can simply replay the same ticket bytes at a later time — and the service server, seeing a well-formed ticket it can decrypt to a valid session key, would accept it without being able to distinguish the replay from the original request. 📄 pp. 310, 316

The authenticator closes this hole. It is a fresh data block — E_K_c,v(ID_c, AD_c, timestamp) — generated per use, encrypted under the session key K_c,v that is embedded in the ticket. To produce a valid authenticator, the presenter must actually know K_c,v, which only the original client does (because only the original client received the TGS reply containing K_c,v). The timestamp inside the authenticator, combined with its very short lifetime (seconds to a minute), prevents replay: the service verifies the timestamp is fresh and rejects stale authenticators. A replay attacker, holding only the ciphertext of the old authenticator, cannot mint a new one with a current timestamp. 📄 pp. 310, 321, 322

  • ☐ Names "ticket replay attack" as the specific vulnerability 📄 pp. 310, 316
  • ☐ Explains attacker sniffs the encrypted ticket but cannot read it 📄 p. 300
  • ☐ States authenticator is encrypted under session key K_c,v 📄 p. 321
  • ☐ Mentions timestamp + short lifetime for freshness 📄 p. 322
  • ☐ Notes only the original client can produce a valid authenticator (knows K_c,v)

Q10. Explain why Kerberos separates the Authentication Server (AS) from the Ticket-Granting Service (TGS), even though both components live inside the same KDC. Your answer should address: (1) what would break if AS and TGS were merged into a single entity that directly authenticates the user every time, (2) how the separation enables the "single logon" property, and (3) how it affects exposure of the user's long-term key K_c.

▶ Show Answer

📝 Model Answer:

(1) Without separation. If AS and TGS were merged such that the KDC re-authenticated the user (via K_c and the password-derived key) every time a service ticket is requested, then the user's long-term key K_c would need to be used — and potentially re-derived from the password — on every service request. This means K_c (or operations involving K_c) happens many times per day, enlarging the window in which password-equivalent material is present in the client's memory and on the wire. It also breaks the clean architectural separation that lets KDC components scale independently. 📄 pp. 302, 306

(2) The "single logon" property. By separating AS from TGS, the user performs the password-based step only once per TGS-ticket lifetime (typically a workday). The AS hands out a TGS ticket + session key K_c,TGS. All subsequent service requests use K_c,TGS (not K_c) to authenticate to TGS. The password itself is used precisely once per day. This is exactly the "single logon" Kerberos is famous for — one authentication grants access to arbitrarily many services. 📄 p. 306

(3) K_c exposure reduction. The client's memory needs to hold K_c (or the password that derives it) only during the brief AS exchange at login. Afterward, K_c can be zeroed from memory; only K_c,TGS and per-service K_c,v are kept live. Compromise of client memory mid-day leaks only session keys — whose validity expires with the ticket — rather than the password-equivalent long-term key. This fundamentally limits the blast radius of a workstation compromise. 📄 pp. 303, 306

🔑 Key Points (Grading Checklist):

  • ☐ Identifies K_c exposure as the central concern 📄 pp. 303, 306
  • ☐ Explains TGS uses K_c,TGS (not K_c) for subsequent authentications 📄 p. 307
  • ☐ Links the separation to "single logon" explicitly 📄 p. 306
  • ☐ Notes ticket lifetime bounds blast radius of session-key compromise 📄 p. 306
  • ☐ Bonus: mentions architectural scalability (AS and TGS loads differ)

Q12. THE #1 MISTAKE STUDENTS MAKE:

Confusing the ticket with the authenticator. The ticket is long-lived (hours, a workday) and reusable — it's the "passport". The authenticator is ephemeral (seconds) and single-use — it's the "fresh selfie" proving the ticket-holder is actually present right now. Without the authenticator, a sniffed ticket can be replayed; the authenticator is what prevents that. They serve opposite temporal roles. 📄 pp. 310, 321, 322
